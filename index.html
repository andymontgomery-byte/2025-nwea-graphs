Student Ability and Growth

NWEA MAP Growth Study — 2025 Norms

Distribution of Math Scores by Grade (Spring)

Each curve shows the distribution of RIT scores for students in that grade

The NWEA MAP allows fair comparisons between students of all ages using a single RIT scale. Notice how the distributions spread out and overlap more in the upper grades, reflecting the wide range of math ability among older students.

Distribution of Reading Scores by Grade (Spring)

Reading growth exhibits fewer disparities compared to math growth, although differences in growth are more pronounced at earlier stages

For reading there is slightly less growth overall compared to math. The distributions compress more tightly in the upper grades, suggesting reading scores converge more than math scores do.

Growth by Subject and Grade

Students improve slightly more in math than they do in reading

Grade schoolers improve around a standard deviation from where they were a year earlier. In other words, the average student improves to the level of an above-average student's scores last year. By high school, growth slows dramatically—the learning rate decreases quickly.

Learning by Percentile, Math

Growth looks different for different students

There is a lot of progress for the best students into and throughout high school. The students in the lowest percentiles don't meaningfully improve their scores after fifth grade. The smarter students will grow faster, widening the achievement gap over time.

Learning by Percentile, Reading

Reading growth exhibits fewer disparities compared to math growth, although differences in growth are more pronounced at earlier stages

For reading there is less overall divergence in the later grades. There is, however, a large difference in the early grades. High-performing young students pull ahead a lot more in reading early on.

The 2025 norms generally shifted lower (fewer RIT points needed to reach a given percentile), reflecting post-pandemic recalibration. The shift varies by grade and subject.
